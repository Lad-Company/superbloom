THE CHALLENGE

* THE BIGGEST BEER BRANDS BOUGHT ATTENTION. VOODOO HAD TO STEAL IT.  
* The craft beer category was drowning in sameness. Every brand was shouting louder, spending more, and making increasingly forgettable ads. Voodoo Ranger had less budget, less distribution, and zero interest in playing the traditional media game. To break through, the brand needed to become impossible to ignore — not by buying attention, but by provoking it.

MODULE 1: Promotional video

---

UNEXPECTED INSIGHT

* OUTRAGE IS THE INTERNET'S LOVE LANGUAGE.  
* People don’t share advertising. They do share ragebait. Especially when it feels too absurd to be fake. The more the stunt blurred the line between parody and reality, the more audiences wanted to participate — turning spectators into investigators, defenders, critics, and co-conspirators. The campaign succeeded because it stopped behaving like marketing and started behaving like culture.

MODULE 2: Press clippings

* Carousel of press

---

THE BIG IDEA

* WE TURNED A PRESS RELEASE INTO A PUBLIC SPECTACLE.  
* So we did something completely irresponsible-looking but low-key brilliant. We announced plans for a fake Voodoo Ranger beer theme park in Napa Valley — complete with permit filings, town meetings, protestors, merch, and a proposed “XTREME BREW FLUME.” What started as a prank quickly spiraled into a public spectacle. Locals debated it. Officials reacted to it. Protestors marched against it. News outlets covered it like it was real. Instead of launching a campaign, we provoked a civic debate And the internet did the rest.

STORY 2: MADE TO BLOOM

COPY:   
Ideas. Talent. Creativity. Reinvention. So much of it depends on the environment, the people around us, the timing, the space we’re given to evolve and sometimes growth means re-rooting entirely. 

In this interview series, we explore this idea through the people who embody it best: creatives who’ve continued to evolve, reinvent, and flourish across different seasons of life and career.

IMAGE: Ana Gasteyer  
**ANA GASTEYER**  
Here is Tony-nominated Schmigadoon\! star, comedy icon, and beloved SNL legend Ana Gasteyer, whose unforgettable characters, razor-sharp wit, and musicality have only grown richer over time. 

Adam: Let’s go deep, fast. What is a moment in your life when you realized something had to change?

Ana: After I left SNL, the logical step would have been to go out to California and that was what was being encouraged by my representatives at the time. Go audition, try to be somebody’s wife on a sitcom, and that was really kind of the extent of the opportunities that I was presented with as a female in the early 2000s. And it just didn’t seem very fulfilling. I knew that I loved Broadway and I had worked a little bit on Broadway during SNL. I went to see two shows in a week. I saw Wonderful Town starring Donna Murphy and I saw the original company of Wicked and I had been a finalist for Wicked early in the show’s early development. I knew that there were some really cool roles for women and as a singer, I had to double down if I was going to make up for lost time, because I had started college as a voice major and gave it all up to do comedy. So I did that. I doubled down, I started training, I started performing at musical theater wherever I possibly could—benefits and concerts and in regional theater—and made that transition very deliberately.

Adam: What was the hardest part about making that change?

Ana: The hardest part was really having to acknowledge that I was kind of starting all over again. At that time (pre-social media), I was really a TV sketch performer, coming into a world I had never really been initiated in. I didn’t know how to dance and I hadn’t studied musical theater formally and I was 35 years old. I was gatecrashing a different industry and understandably, people on stage were suspicious of a television actress coming in and stealing their thunder and so I did feel like I really had to earn my stripes

Adam: More growth stuff- was there a period where you felt stuck between who you were and who you were becoming?

Ana: Absolutely. People were interested in seeing me for comedy roles, but I really felt like I had to prove I could do the vocal part and had to really dig deep to do that. I had to study, I had to sing, I had to really stretch my own comfort zone because singers at the level of Idina Menzel and Shoshana Bean—people I was in the room with—were people who had been refining that talent and actual physical musculature for some time. So I definitely felt like I have to prove that I can do this in order to make it to the next level. That’s why I focused so incredibly hard on Elphaba, because I knew if I could sing Elphaba, and if people could hear me sing Elphaba and know, in the Broadway community, that I was capable of singing Elphaba, that I would come to the other side.  

Adam: What did success mean earlier in your career and what does it mean to you now?

Ana: Success was much more in the eyes of other people earlier in my career. So much of those early moves are about being accepted from the outside and later in your career, success is much more defined around personal goals. It’s way less important to me what other people assess as success because I also know that they’re not always going to see it the way that I do, and it’s much more about the goals that I set for myself. Things I want to learn, things I want to challenge, challenges I set up for myself, things I want to write, things I want to produce, people I want to work with- obviously there’s a lot more agency in that. The nice thing about later in your career is, theoretically, you do have a track record and you do have evidence that what you’re doing is meaningful. Earlier in your career, it’s a little bit more of just a lark and a wildcard because you’re kind of throwing peas at the ceiling and the ones that stick count. I mean, obviously I knew I was going to work in comedy, but my first television shows were Seinfeld and a police drama called NYPD Blue—just little parts. So it kind of could have broken any way and the way that does is the one that ends up being the one that defines you.

Adam: What is something you had to let go of in order to move forward?

Ana: I had to let go of the fact that I was going to be a conventional idea to someone. Early on, I knew I was going to be on an independent track because I didn’t look like what I thought of as a TV actress. I was told all the time, “Oh, you’re not going to work in TV because you’re not blonde or the girl next door.” You don’t look like Courtney Thorne-Smith, you don’t look like this blonde or that blonde, which is so funny because now I think I look so conventional by comparison. I had to let go of trying to squinch into that mold. I was just this morning thinking about how I’d auditioned for Modern Family and what a joke that was in retrospect. I had to let go of waiting for other people to make things for me and I had to make them for myself, and it’s so much more satisfying to me to have done that.

Adam: What’s something that people misunderstand about growth or reinvention?

Ana: I mean, I don’t know that this is something people misunderstand. I do think that during this phase, you have to live in a place of uncertainty and that’s really hard when you’re a grown-up. We can live in a place of uncertainty a lot as kids, but we don’t do it as grown-ups- we don’t sort of wade into waters we’re not familiar with without a fair amount of conviction that that’s going to go well for us. More than misunderstand, I think people just forget. It’s just a lot of admitting that you don’t know everything. In becoming a musical theater performer, that is what I had to learn. It’s a very, very carefully studied and refinement-oriented skill set, and I had to just kind of admit that I didn’t know a lot of things and that humility was super grounding.

Adam: What’s one thing you know now that you wish you had understood earlier?

Ana: That it really has nothing to do with other people. I wish I understood earlier how much of it is not in your control and you can only do what you can do and worry about the components that you’re capable of.  

It took me a long time to figure out that the harder you work and how much you put into an audition or a pitch, is actually what ends up being the most satisfying. If you feel like you’ve thrown your best self into it, you always get something from it later- whether it’s knowledge or confidence or relationships, you learn and you grow from that experience. Sometimes, the hard work can be paid forward, where somebody who’s in a position of power might think, “Wow, they always do a good job and I’m going to reward that eventually.”  

I certainly also wish I’d spent less time worrying about all the things I wasn’t- it doesn’t help you do the best version you can do and it took me a long time to figure that out. 

IMAGE: Tom Pace  
**TOM PACE**   
Tom is a nomadic, multi-hyphenate creative force who is the fertilizer for some of Hollywood’s sharpest film marketing.  
Adam: Where did you first “take root” in your life, your career, and what did that environment give you?

Tom: I guess my hard-to-define career in entertainment took root with my very first gig in Los Angeles at TriStar Pictures as the assistant story editor.  I had an office, handled all the script and novel coverage to help find potential films, I tooled around the Sony lot on a golf cart and lorded over the vast script library. As part of the development team, I got a firsthand look at how a big studio helps turn a story into a motion picture. I also got to see how a big studio can quickly derail a brilliant story – chopping away at the script until nothing is left.  It was both exhilarating and oppressive at the same time.   

Adam: At what point did you realize your environment was shaping you—for better or worse?

Tom: I think we all dream of being disruptors in our chosen field – of really shaking things up, rewiring the system on our way up the ladder.  I brought a different kind of disruption to my first job as assistant story editor.  No, I managed to ‘disrupt’ by being a complete disaster at my job.  At one point I was put on probation for distributing the Godzilla script with 20 pages missing.  Also, the creative in me found reviewing and rejecting other writer’s scripts and novels downright traitorous so after almost 3 years it was time to move on.  

Adam: What did you learn early on that you had to later unlearn?

Tom: Pulling up roots from a film studio’s story department you need to step away from judgement or other work and begin inhabiting your own creative space.  Replanting yourself as a copywriter in advertising and in the movie and TV promo world after story editing at a studio means unlearning storytelling that takes 90 pages and replacing it with 30 seconds of copy.  

Adam: Something creatives don’t often talk about is our evolution- when we outgrow our initial containers and start to grow in other places.  When was this for you?

Tom: I stayed for 3 years at the studio.  The development team was probably happy to see me go and I jumped directly onto a big-budget Hollywood movie starring Robin Williams.  I was the director’s assistant and I learned about production, worked with the director on the screenplay, casting, storyboards, and got a crash course making the best cappuccino in Hollywood. I think immersing myself in the politics and pitfalls of making a $100 million dollar art house film really drove home my desire to be a freelance creative, no matter the cost.  Emphasis on free (or so I thought).  

I think like many struggling creatives, it was undiagnosed ADHD, a truly restless mind, and my total lack of organizational skills that inspired me to attempt freelance writing.  

Disruption

Adam: What forced the change was it your choice, or did something push you?

Tom: I’ve switched paths so often that it’s hard to remember what forced the change – but each time I think I believed there was a better opportunity ahead.  The question isn’t how much did it cost to pull up roots and try something new.  The question should be how much do you think it would have cost you to stay in one place?  How much would you be sacrificing by not leaving?  To me the cost of staying always outweighed the risks of cutting loose. 

Transplant Shock \- How was it at first

Adam: What was harder than you expected after making the change?

Tom: Leaving behind a cush studio job and stepping away from that track feel exhilarating.  But I quickly figured out I needed to be drought resistant, which sometimes meant swallowing my pride.  Jumping from marketing a hit TV show to working with big ad agencies on national campaigns and churning out content for the Disney Channel.  If you’re looking for a plant metaphor, I was a sunflower, bending towards the sun.  Wherever the work was, that’s where you’d find me.  

What was harder?  Learning to be resilient when jumping from uncertain freelance gig to the next.  I tried my hand at advertising, marketing, creative directing, I directed some spots for PBS Kids, even launched a non-profit during the pandemic with some friends to help restaurants.  I discovered I excelled at naming things – a random gift, from naming ice cream flavors for a Bangkok ice cream shop to the coming up with the title of a WW11 novel to the name of a Tom Hanks movie for Apple.  

Finding the Right Soil \- aka conditions

Adam: When did things start to click in the new environment?

Tom: I think my undefinable career started making sense when I realized all the connections I’d made over the years started feeling purposeful.  When all my experiences, from travel to music to making art to interacting with my wonderfully eclectic social circle, began informing my creativity in a natural way, I knew I was onto something.  Instead of the world passing you by, you’re growing with it, evolving.  

The soil is richest when you let it breathe.  When you turn it over.  Being a freelance writer/creative consultant affords me the breathing room that is crucial if you want to continue delivering inspired work.  Being part of a wildly diverse roster of projects, it’s like rotating crops to keep the soil healthy.  It’s good for the brain.  

What specifically made this place different from where you were before? What conditions do you now realize you need to thrive?

Growth \+ Bloom

Adam: What does “thriving” actually look like for you now?

Tom: For me thriving is being able to work on projects that I am proud to be a part of.  Success for me is being able to put all my life experiences into play when brainstorming a creative solution to market a film or launch a beauty brand for a friend.  As a movie buff first and foremost, despite my background in film development, television and movie marketing, I see the world first and foremost through the eyes of a fan.  Being able to apply that fan-fueled creative helps me avoid some of the mistakes we used to make at the studio level.  It’s a different world now but some of those lessons still apply.  

Adam: What are you able to do here that you couldn’t do before?

Tom: Being a “Thought Partner” for major studio film releases affords me the chance to put my ideas into action, working alongside editors to bring trailers and digital content to life.  The bigger the movie, the larger the team working to market the film.  I think what I can do now that I couldn’t do maybe 5 years ago is get my ideas to the front of the line – test them out in the editing bay, see if anything sticks instead of having them die on the vine.  It’s amazing to watch an idea take root and grow.  

Adam: How has your definition of success changed?

Tom: I used to think success meant selling your screenplay and seeing it land in 3,700 theaters on a Friday.  I quickly learned that being part of the team that finds a way to get the audience to connect with a movie or a TV series can be rewarding in its own way.  Thriving right now for me is being able to contribute ideas to the marketing of movies like “One Battle After Another”, “The Long Walk”, “Dune”, and “Sinners” and being able to watch those films go on to great acclaim and success.  

Reflection \+ Advice

Adam: Looking back, what would have happened if you stayed?

Tom: If I had stayed at the studio instead of breaking free to take on all these diverse roles and projects and experience the successes (and the failures) – I like to think I would have found a way to help nurture incredible projects into being.  But my path would have been more direct – without all the side missions, side-hustles and sidetracking.  The best part about a career as a freelancer is the ‘free’ part.  The lancing is painful, but the freedom tastes so good.  

Adam: What do people misunderstand about “starting over” or changing paths?

Tom: When something isn’t working it might seem scary to pull up roots and try something new but It’s not as scary as continuing to do something you aren’t loving.  I’ve had to change paths 100 times and each time I follow the sun. You don’t want to be that person ‘tolerhating’ their job.  Don’t be a ‘tolerhater’.  

Adam: What advice would you give to someone who feels stuck but is afraid to leave?

Tom: I’m in no position to give advice since my career choices have been more reactionary than purposeful.  It’s a ride that was tailored to my skills and my desire for freedom – I think the best advice I can give someone wanting to forge a path as an independent freelance creative is to experience as much of the world as you can  but stay grounded through great friends – hydrate as much as possible – and follow the sun.  The work will follow.     

IMAGE: James Cox  
**JAMES COX**  
James is a Laurel Canyon polymath writer, director, and novelist, whose work is rooted in Hollywood myth-making, excess, ambition and technological future state. 

Briony: James, can I please start our interview by shamelessly fangirling for a brief moment. \_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_ I feel better. As in every garden, creativity begins as a seed.  Where did you first take root in your career?

James: I was that kid with a camcorder making movies in my backyard when I was 10 years old and with a  little deviation, it was a straight shot for me from high school movies to Berkeley to NYU. I was a cinematographer first who leaped over into directing and I wrote this short film called Atomic Tabasco that blew me up—I came out of film school with a studio deal. 

But it was my second picture, Wonderland with Val Kilmer, where it all came together for me. This incredible experience of directing Val as he transformed into John Holmes. 

I remember being on set, and my head was on fire with creativity, ideas that could blossom in so many different directions—be it with the camera, an actor’s performance, writing a new piece of improvised dialogue. 

I was home. 

Briony: I bet a lot of film directors would be envious of your flying start, James but is there a project that maybe didn’t grow in the way you had planned?

James: Cut to 15 years after Wonderland and I’m directing another notorious Los Angeles true crime picture called The Billionaire Boys Club, starring Taron Egerton, Emma Roberts and Kevin Spacey. A tremendously talented actor, and ironically, a high school theater friend of Val’s. I’d put eight years into that script; the crew killed themselves; we shot over the holidays. In post forever, we literally had a bidding war brewing right after screening for buyers when the whole scandal hit and both Kevin and my movie went radioactive. 

Stunned was an understatement. 

I was reeling. 

I think in one week, I was in three different therapists’ offices, just asking myself…

Is this it?

Briony: Okay, are you sure this interview isn’t your next screenplay?  How did this out-of-your-control circumstance help you shift perspective and find a new playground?

James: I knew that I was done with true crime, with historical, fact-based drama.  Maybe I was just done, period.  So I retreated into the cave...’cuz something had been brewing.

In the back of my mind, I had this idea, nothing more, really, than a scene, and I didn’t really know where it was gonna go. But I just started writing.

That first scene turned into a series of character bios that grew into short stories. Then, this whole world started to blossom around a heist that went down in the fall of 2051, a dystopian future with bots and brain computer interfaces straight outta Blade Runner or The Matrix. 

And what I thought was going to be a script for my next movie called GTAI just kept growing and growing, thanks to a number of key nudges along the way. The script turned into a manuscript that I sent to a manager who sold it to Blackstone Publishing, who bought the book and its sequel.  Now I was an author with a 2-book publishing deal.

Briony: Wait, I did not see that coming. So now we are in an entirely new medium. We are obsessed with the written word here at SuperBloom. What did you come to learn about publishing? 

James: Going through editorial with a professional editor, copy editor, and proof editor from a major publisher was a trial by fire, for sure. I’d never even heard of the Chicago Manual of Style until I found out how many of its rules I’d broken. 

There’s this thing they say with a book, if you don’t agree with a note, they’re like, “Look, it’s your book; you have to be happy with it.” Whereas in screenwriting, you get a note from a producer you disagree with, you fight for your idea, and maybe you prove your point, but if you don’t, you either do the note… or they fire you and get someone who will. And finally she says, “Well, It is your book. But. That being said. If your reader gets confused by a passage in your book for, say, the third time, causes him or her not to flip back but just throw it in the trash, never finish it, and never tell anyone to read your book. If that matters to you? Then you might want to listen to this note.”

And I just started laughing, and I was like, “Okay, now we’re talking.” Right? And I think that was when our real collaboration began. 

But the real baptism was Book Two. They say they’re harder, and it was the hardest thing I’ve ever done, my Mt. Everest learning curve for sure. The first book had a thousand handlers, feeding me encouragement from one draft to another—but Book Two, I was all alone. Grand Theft AI: PRISM Break is a big, aggressive, ambitious idea. No template or a model. It’s a futuristic prison break, but it’s told from the outside crew breaking in, like a heist but the loot is the hero of Book 1 who’s now trapped inside this meatgrinder of a prison. I ended up writing it two and half times, kept getting it wrong, roaming around in the dark, like I was writing my first screenplay all over again.

I ended up turning in the manuscript a couple weeks before my daughter was born. 

Briony: So at this point, you are a fully fledged author. What quick sand condition did you need to find your creative voice and style?

James: The “quickening” for me, the final culmination when I finally felt myself bloom, was just last fall, going through the editorial for Book 2\. I had published Grand Theft AI, done readings, book fairs, panels, lectures, and written several short stories since starting PRISM Break back in 2021\. My voice had grown from coming out of the gates hot, flexing its muscles wherever it could, to maybe maturing with a little discipline and restraint—you know, fewest words possible. So by the time I was doing the final revisions, there were passages written in the last three weeks of the book that I’d had in my mind for years, but never knew how or even if they’d find their way into the book. Especially in the last chapter—the book’s built like Empire Strikes Back, a mid-act, fucked-at-the beginning-fucked-at-the-end, which is risky, ‘cuz you’re gambling the reader’s gonna be hooked enough to come back for a third, you know? And it really came down to hitting that last note of hope right—not too deliberate, too obvious, too artificial, but when it landed, and I was banging that thing right down to the wire—it just fucking opened up the world in a way that I’d realized I’d been driving towards for years, never knowing exactly how I was gonna get there until I’d stuck the landing.

Briony: What advice would you give to someone who feels a bit stuck in the mud? Look at me Kimmie.

James: Looking back, if I have any advice as to the who, what, where, when, etc of making a major change, it’s this: Follow your voice. 

If you get to a place where your voice just isn’t yours anymore... 

I don’t mean indulgently yours. 

But a place where the compromises have diluted your voice to a point where it can’t differentiate itself—you can’t enunciate with any ownership—then follow it where it needs to go. Because that means it’s loud enough for you to know it’s being suppressed and you’ll know what it sounds and feels like when it’s let loose.

I’m not the guy who looked around at my environment, recognized that I needed to leave and made a conscious effort to exit stage right. I was just well-aware that our medium was and is under siege—generational change, an understatement—during this massive technological and cultural upheaval, we can’t predict or control how our voice will reach an audience. But I was sure of one thing: You can be damn sure the story is universal. 

And we’ll be telling and listening to stories for as long as there is fire. 

So trust your voice. Have the courage to follow your voice. 

And it will lead you to fertile soil.

WATER YOUR CORPSES  
Terrifying musings from our Michael Wolf, who has found a sunny space for shady stories inside SuperBlooms’ Creative Collective.   
Artwork by Caitlin Hickey

I’ve never been particularly afraid of death.

Life, however, is a much more gripping terror. An incessant swarm of decisions buzzing daily in the anxious cavities of my skull. Tears welling at the kindergarten bus stop as I asked my sister ‘what if I’m jesus?’; A perverse narcissism of self-important shame that doesn’t think myself a worthy messiah but fears I’ll fail the savior stunt strapped to my sweaty soul.

One wrong step and a life is undone. And then, the gorgeous punchline, you’re forced to live it out. Death is unavoidable, easy, often happening with very little foresight or effort. Then, blessings, you and your worries evaporate, no dust to sweep, the documents left for someone else to sort. But life condenses down upon your brow, growing heavy and knitted as the condensation pools and you slowly drown in droplets.

Against medical advice, I have stopped taking my SSRIs.

I wonder, what could I possibly say to the advertising world about failure? Did any of us grow up yearning to craft spots? Did our dreams and notebook doodles fall neatly into six, fifteen, and thirty second formats? I’d bet my umbilical cord (still wrapped around my neck) that anyone reading these words has at least one 512gb LaCie Rugged trapping the genie of your college short film, your one-draft-from-finished screenplay, or the masters of a garageband rap rock epic that truly might have revived the genre. How many times a day does that gaudy orange flesh lamp catch your eye and beg to be rubbed? We could have been DJs, the lot of us\! If not for those persistent student loans or ‘lovely’ children that we’re so ‘grateful’ we had. Kids are great, but has your offspring ever offered you MDMA and afters at Berghain?

I don’t sound bitter, do I?

Let’s hope not. Advertising saved my life. Some of my closest friends were made while crafting short form hot dog content. Some of the work I’m proudest of revolved around directing a trained opossum to try and sell rugs. Advertising has taken me around the world and paid for my passion projects and healthcare. 

...And yet, work is work and art is art. And art takes work and work takes art. To crack another window, flowers grow from corpses. 

I am a prolific gardener. In the sense that I have nurtured hundreds of gorgeous plants to death. The body count of fiddle leaves lifeless upon my hardwood floor would make Patton weep. Nevertheless, I persisted. I drag my botanical horse to water and force it to drink (possible cause of my root rot?). Even surrounded by wilting, my heart leaps. In all of these ceramic caskets...  dirt. A seemingly inert nothing that sleeps beneath our feet. And yet, there’s magic in the soil. Not all, mind you. Some are barren, arid, depleted. But the best, the most life-giving comes from compost. Decomposing plant and food matter, organic detritus, and literal shit. As it sits and steeps, bacteria and fungus feed upon the rot.

fuck, i’m turned on

Somewhere in this process, highly scientific and researchable “things” happen (which I will spare us both from learning), and the dirt becomes nutrient-rich fertilizer. Food for seeds. Grounds for growth. The goddamn cycle of life.

This is how we must view our work. Some art blossoms and thrives. But the projects that wither on the vine are just as precious. They mustn’t be discarded, but tossed, with care, upon the compost heap to mingle and decompose with every other fetid petal until the very molecules of the ideas are inextricable. Compost piles smell like ripe shit, literally hot and wet. The instinct is to avoid them. Take the long route round. But doesn’t manure...kind of smell good? Isn’t that fecund offal brimming with more life than your blank page? How can we expect to compose if we don’t first decompose?

Anyone who has composted knows that it takes time. You have to balance out your clippings and food stuffs. You have to turn and churn the pile. It doesn’t just happen. My first attempt produced a wasps nest inside a barrel of brown banana peels and many high pitched screams. This is to say, our failures won’t be reborn unless we tend to them.

College began with a whimper as my pre-med dreams were dashed upon the rocky shores of second semester Organic Chemistry. However, a stem sprouted from the dirt in the form of the best job I’ve ever had; acting as an elderly man for nurse training simulations. Three days a week I’d grab my white wig, tweed jacket, and walking cane to pretend to be a geriatric husband visiting his wife in the recovery room. My job was to be as annoying and distracting as possible as the nurses-to-be tried to treat my ailing wife. When, like clockwork, smoke poured under the door and the fire alarm blared, I’d panic, screaming at the nurses to save my wife. I’d then stumble and crash to the cold floor, the blood packets hidden in my cheeks spraying across the sterilized linoleum, as the nurses wheeled the patient beds around my lifeless body. Over the course of two years, no one ever saved me. I was left to burn alive every time. I like to think that I’m partially responsible for failing dozens of aspiring nurses who just didn’t have the bedside manner. While this job eventually flat lined, it brought me to short-form improv comedy, the only true art form.

It was then swiftly unbooked when she re-read my email and realized that I was not late night writer and White House Correspondents’ Dinner comedian Michelle Wolf but the plucky unknown upstart Michael Wolf. The concept festered until we found a new venue, then a podcast format, then a script stage play, and eventually, a decade later performed a new, final version at The Kennedy Center. 

Regrettably, The Kennedy Center has recently undergone some administrative changes, and I’m no longer as eager to wear the free hat I received bearing their logo.

I could go on listing my failures, and so I will\! Double time. The three achingly earnest albums of sob-folk music, too easily searchable on bandcamp. The slam poetry endeavor that started with journal entries screamed over post-rock and ended at the Bowery Ballroom when I ruined Keifer Sutherland’s date night. My promising cross country career, a shiny junior high runner, a tangle of long limbs in a silky singlet. They say I had promise until my coach called me fat and made me realize, for the very first time, that I owned a body (flawed). The absurdist web series where I cleverly cashed favors and cast multiple future Emmy and Oscar winners just a few years too early for it to do any good. Playing house in New Jersey for a semblance of stability, all of which crumbled when I could no longer talk golf and lawns to the neighbors. A promising pilot pitch with every meeting canceled by the strike. 

And even now, as I write this, I sit in a strange sublet surrounded by unpacked boxes of my life, struck dumb by the heat-lightning end of decade-long relationship, my first marriage, my only tether to the human world. New hormones pump through my blood because boy was yet another valiant attempt that never quite panned out. Even though I’m too afraid to say it, too afraid to tell anyone, let alone my co-workers (until this moment. Oops. Sorry\!).

Today these defeats don’t feel as potent. Even a year ago I might have wailed and gnashed my teeth, rattled my fists at the dark storm clouds of heaven, and torn my flesh to ribbons (Note: I am not dramatic). Maybe it’s the fresh chemicals, maybe it’s the thousands in therapy, maybe it’s Maybelline (sponcon), but I’m starting to warm up to life. And life, it seems, is spent in the graveyard. Microdosing death by fucking up my life again and again and again. Burying the bodies. Each one wears my face. But I will tend these graves. I will honor the dead. I will kneel and pray at the tombstones of my failures that the worms do their work and my corpses turn to fertile compost. My sundress flutters obscenely around my ankles. I will tend my garden.

In time, perhaps next spring, we’ll stroll between the plots and spot the thin green stems of something new sprouting from remains.

STORY 4:1-800-FLOWERS

IMAGE: Thomas Payne

1-800-FLOWERS  
Meet the botanical stewards who keep the Wild Flower Hotline ringing.  
By Juan Velasquez

Artwork courtesy of Theodore Payne Foundation

“There’s a hotline for wildflowers? Really?” was podcaster Dave Stachowiak’s initial reaction when he heard that his friend Tom Henschel would be narrating the Wild Flower Hotline, a podcast about plants… or something. A “hotline,” for those born after the internet—and for the purposes of this article—is a number you’d call to get all sorts of help, from movie showtimes and mental health support to tips on how to beat a difficult video game, and, thanks to the Theodore Payne Foundation, where to spot the season’s wildflower blooms.

Stachowiak admits that wildflowers weren’t something he was especially excited about until he tuned in to listen to his friend’s curious new voiceover gig. “I found so much delight and joy, both in listening to \[Henschel’s\] voice—because he has an amazing voice—and in the passion and joy he brings to something I would otherwise never think about,” he says via Zoom from his podcast studio. That sense of unexpected delight is the result of decades of work by the Theodore Payne Foundation.

Located 15 miles northwest of downtown Los Angeles in Sun Valley, the nonprofit founded the hotline in 1983 and has run it each spring during wildflower bloom season ever since. Callers can check in weekly and listen to a voice recording explaining where—always on public land—they can find myriad California native wildflowers. Over the decades, the hotline has evolved: you can now listen online wherever you get your podcasts —worry not, you can still dial 818-768-1802 ext. 7 to hear it the old-fashioned way— but the mission has stayed the same.

Former Theodore Payne staffer Andrew Chaves explains the mission beautifully: “The Wild Flower Hotline is an educational resource that creates connections rather than simply delivering random facts about plants. It’s not marketing. It’s stewardship as a voicemail.”

On a sunny, slightly crisp Earth Day—an especially fitting backdrop—I visited the Theodore Payne Foundation and spoke with executive director Amy Greenwood. She noted that while Instagram and TikTok content can inspire a love of nature, it can also create a false sense of having already experienced it, even if only virtually. The Wild Flower Hotline, she argues, helps push people beyond passive scrolling and encourages them to actually go outside and encounter nature firsthand.

Greenwood is right—Instagram has commodified the beauty of the bloom. Take, for example, the hundreds of thousands of posts of the California poppy “superbloom” in the Antelope Valley. These places have become touristy, oversaturated, and influencer fodder. People go, trample flowers to get their selfies, post, and leave. 

Nature need not be gatekept; far from it, people should enjoy it, but often without knowledge of where they are or the ramifications of interacting with these areas.

The weekly report and the folks at the Theodore Payne Foundation strive for reverence, not exploitation. Often, they highlight lesser-known locations, helping to draw attention away from the algorithm’s bloom du jour destinations and ease pressure on already oversaturated spots. The hotline doesn’t want to “blow up the spot,” but rather to “blow up the care,” as Genevieve Arnold, TPF’s seed and conservation program manager, so deftly puts it.

Beyond encouraging listeners to physically encounter nature, Arnold explains that education and advocacy around these native plants make those experiences more personally enriching for even the casual wildflower admirer. “What the Wild Flower Hotline does is give people the opportunity to go one step deeper and say, ‘Okay, not just where does this flower grow, but what is this place called? How do I get there? Is this part of a national park? Is this a preserve? What protections are in place for this area?’” she says, sitting in a swivel chair in front of her workspace.

Knowledge deepens connection. To know these plants is to love them, and to love them is to want to protect them. The hotline provides that crucial context. Arnold says the Wild Flower Hotline deepens people’s connection to a place by giving names to what they’ll see, preparing them to recognize the landscape as they arrive. “You’re taking it to that deeper level of remembering,” she adds. “It goes beyond stopping and taking selfies.”

Across from Arnold and myself sits Katie Tilford, director of development and communications—my guide and the key to unlocking TPF’s wonderfully nerdy botanical world. She’s been instrumental in connecting me with the people behind the foundation and the Wild Flower Hotline. In the midst of waxing poetic on all things wildflower, she makes an excellent point: the ultimate goal of the hotline and TPF is to inspire people to care about the outdoors and take an active role in conserving it, especially in a world that often seems determined to destroy it. “Preservation takes everyday people, not just scientists, botanists, or biologists. And that’s what the Wild Flower Hotline gives you—a person.”

That person Tilford is referring to is none other than Tom Henschel, a self-described “accidental gardener” and the narrator of the Wild Flower Hotline since 2023\. A podcaster in his own right, Henschel’s timbre is measured, present, and soothing—imagine a more casual David Attenborough. His vocal cadence feels like: “You’re safe, pay attention, I’ve got you.” When you tune in, you hear him recite plant names such as chaparral snapdragon, cardinal catchfly, scarlet larkspur, and showy penstemon—playful, phonetically labyrinthine puzzle pieces that Henschel snaps together week after week. Through a laptop screen, Henschel says, “Before I record the hotline every week, I have two things in my heart: joy and wonder.” Smiling, he adds, “I’m excited to read and imagine it all. To talk about colors, textures, and smells. It’s awesome.”

There is something about the old-time charm of the hotline that resonates with listeners. “There’s something deeply grounding about the corporeal nature of the hotline,” Chaves agrees. That grounding comes from Henschel’s personal elation and vocal delivery. Chaves goes further, saying, “The literal sound of it is meditative. I think it’s also the reason why some people listen to NPR.” Listening to the recordings as a purely audio artifact is reason enough to tune in. Henschel tells me that a TPF staffer’s sister, who had moved to Helsinki, would listen in to feel “at home and grounded in California” while living abroad. “I have no idea who’s listening, but I know if they’re listening, it’s meaningful to them,” he says with conviction.

But the Theodore Payne Foundation does know who listens. On a phone call, Chaves—who worked at TPF from 2011 to 2023—explained that the hotline did go through a change in listenership during the pandemic. No longer was it just a niche report for botanists and nerdy hikers, but it took on a more culturally visible role. He attributes this to the rise in people wanting to get out of their homes and have “low-risk” outdoor adventures. The reset of the lockdown era, coupled with an ongoing online malaise many people are experiencing today, speaks to the renewed interest in the hotline’s, for lack of a better word, purity. “Everybody lives in a world of urgency. The hotline is there to help people be mindful, slow down, and notice a flower on the side of the freeway,” Chaves says.

One of these newer post-pandemic listeners is Sara Seidman, a millennial Angeleno who has become a Wild Flower Hotline devotee. She’s artistic and has niche interests—someone who reads Atlas Obscura to find places off, off, off the beaten path. It’s a sensibility that fits Los Angeles’ driving culture, especially for someone with a bumper sticker that reads: “Keep Honking\! I’m Listening to the Wild Flower Hotline.”

Of the hotline, she says, “It’s very calm and soothing. There is a low-key excitement about these small flowers that you can see on the side of the road.” Seidman has learned a great deal about native plants: where to find them near her home and how to pronounce many of them (some are quite the mouthful). But she stresses, “It’s not just information; it’s an invitation. An invitation to explore,” she says on a call.

LA residents exploring flora in their own backyards is exactly what Wild Flower Hotline writer Lorrae Fuentes has in mind when penning her weekly botanical wrap-up. A botanist, educator, and member of the California Native Plant Society, Fuentes has been writing the hotline reports since 2011\. She is evangelical about finding natural beauty in Southern California’s most unlikely places, from backyard gardens to the marshlands around LAX, which serve as butterfly habitat. “The natural history is right there in their urban area. They don’t have to go far,” she says.

For Fuentes, the work that TPF and the Wild Flower Hotline take on is not just informational, but existential as well. In her writing, Fuentes strives to preserve the breathtaking and complicated landscape of California for future generations. “Climate change is a threat. People think it’s happening so slowly, but they’re not really engaged in conservation or what climate change really is,” she warns. 

Fuentes pointed to this spring as a vivid example: early March brought a wave of reports about unusually early blooms in warm weather, only for a sudden heatwave and lack of rain to dry out areas just weeks later. That close attention to small environmental changes, drawn from weekly reports and anecdotes she gathers, makes the hotline a kind of microcosm of larger ecological volatility. 

The devastating Eaton and Palisades fires, along with government layoffs and cuts to parks and ecological programs—areas the current administration appears intent on stripping back—made 2025 a difficult year for California ecology as well as for the hotline. Tilford explains that many of those who contribute reports are national and state park employees; without staff in the field during peak bloom season, those observations simply don’t come in, leaving noticeable gaps in the hotline’s weekly record.

Zooming out, Tilford notes a persistent misunderstanding: that nature is self-sustaining, requiring no human care. In reality, she says, people have been cultivating and tending landscapes since the dawn of history, and that work remains essential rather than optional.

With the administration spending vast sums on “war, police, and ICE,” Tilford adds, “The money that is being asked for parks and open space is so minuscule. But the rewards we get back from it are immeasurable and priceless.”

Even as those pressures accumulate—fires reshaping landscapes, funding gaps limiting what can be recorded—the hotline continues its quiet work of translating ecological disruption into something individuals can actually perceive. The report is a weekly reminder that landscapes are changing, but not absent; damaged, but not silent.

For Seidman, that reminder became tangible on a drive to Figueroa Mountain in Santa Barbara County, a place she had first discovered through the Wild Flower Hotline. The area had burned in a 2024 fire, but a recent report noted it had reopened to the public. “It was so cool to listen to the hotline and see all the wildflowers that were coming back,” she says.

Driving through, she encountered not just a landscape marked by fire, but one quietly returning to life.

STORY 5: WHO GETS THE LIGHT

IMAGE: Who Get the light

WHO GETS THE LIGHT?

Why some careers flourish in full sun, while others are quietly kept in the shade.  
By Christina Connerton

Artwork by Courtney Wells

Advertising is like a special little club. So much of what happens inside of it is so uniquely it. And within it, we’ve established our norms–late night client dinners, early morning testing results, pitch decks, production timelines, tightening budgets, faster, more, now. It’s made us experts at problem solving on the fly. It’s why creatives flourish here; we thrive in the controlled chaos. 

As someone who was late-diagnosed (and I mean, compellingly, alarmingly late) with ADHD, the reliability of our go-go-go-make-make-make-now-now-now mentality gives me the dopamine hits my silly, fleshy, little brain requires. 

But. 

Like with anything, when norms are established, changes to the norm are often ignored. If something is working, why change it? If it’s not broke and all that. And with the reins being tightened in every direction, brands fearing audience alienation, and a tenuous political climate–it’s a touch Third Reich out there–change feels a whole lot like risk. We just don’t have the time or the money or the mental capacity for anything other than a done deal. 

But we’re all creative, right? That’s like our thing. We don’t shut up about creativity being king. We lament about it, brag about it, write sonnets on LinkedIn about it. We’re like one bad pitch away from quoting Taylor Swift’s Eldest Daughter about our terminal uniqueness and drowning ourselves in a Mad Men-style martini about it. (I find us annoyingly adorable.)

So much of creating relies on us breaking norms, even when it’s inconvenient, even when it comes with a cost. And sometimes the norm we have to break is our own comfort, our own superiority, our own ways of working.

What I’m talking about is visibility politics. At a time when the norm (read: WASP) is more in style than ever and DEI is being systematically gutted, we, as creative leaders in this industry and in pop culture, need to push back. Sometimes that means actively pursuing progress within our field and in the work we put out. It means shining a light on people who have been historically marginalized and letting them bloom.

\*\*

I didn’t think I’d end up in advertising. 

When I was 21 I had that kind of art school arrogance that can only be bought and cultivated for $60,000/year (dance major at NYU, what’s up). A coworker at my serving job (did you hear me? I said dance major at NYU) was bartending after having lost his first-ever design job at an advertising agency due to an unexpected illness taking over his life. 

By the time I met him, his health was back to normal but his career path was not. And he talked about his former job a lot. At the time, I didn’t have the worldliness to truly understand the intersection of disability and creativity–and to be fair, neither did he. Instead, he focused on making sure we knew he was an art director, and an artist, and a really hard worker, and definitely not going to be a bartender forever. And I focused on how annoying all of that was. 

At that point in time, I was a healthy dancer slash server slash canonically heartbroken poet. And, of course, a white woman (still that). The inherent privilege I had in my body meant that I could walk around believing myself a radical without ever really needing to confront what that meant. My worldview was limited to what I had always known to be immovable.

Things changed, though. I switched careers in 2016 and moved to LA to work as a production coordinator at an ad agency. I spent enough time bugging people with increasingly desperate quips like “hey did you know I’m a writer, too?” and “I wrote really bad poetry about an ex you could take a look at,” that eventually I was asked to work on a pitch deck, then a pitch, then a campaign.

I came into my own as a creative in advertising in 2018 at exactly the same time I became disabled by chronic illness. And honestly? I am so lucky it happened that way. 

Not because I’m weird or optimistic (the universe cannot have a divine plan that involves me undergoing annual colonoscopies, I’m sorry) but because to move through the world disabled–or as anything other than white, cis, straight, male, able-bodied, housed, christian-ish–requires adaptability and creativity to navigate systems that often do not bend to, make room for, or accommodate you. (Unless, of course, you work at SuperBloom, which accommodates and makes room for all of my everything.)

Creativity became the worldview–like when people go and do ayahuasca and say they’ve been enlightened, except real. 

\*\*

Molly Burke, a blind disability activist and influencer, once explained how we attempt to cure blindness and it really put things into perspective for me. There are like–I don’t know–50, 100, 200 ways you can go blind. (I tried to find the exact number. All I got is that it’s a lot.) Now, it’s the inclination of the public to invest in finding a cure for blindness–a noble and worthy cause. However, knowing that there are many, many different causes, it also seems reflected in the science that there are many, many different ways to tackle finding their potential cures. And while there are thousands of studies every year focused on vision restoration, there is still no specific cure for blindness. And yet, we persist. 

You know what we don’t attempt with equal sincerity, despite how simple it would be to implement alongside these scientific efforts? 

Image descriptions on social media posts so blind users utilizing screen readers (which can pick up captions but cannot decipher what’s in an image) can understand what’s posted.

It sounds so silly. It’s just social media. It’s not like finding a cure for blindness or anything. But… you use it every day, right? We market on it all year long. The majority of the work I do ends up only on socials. Have you ever had a client require an image description in their captions? I haven’t. Have you ever asked for it to be included? I haven’t. 

This simple accommodation not only helps our blind audiences have access to social media, but it helps advertisers get their (our\!) message across to more people. According to the CDC, there are over 7 million people in the United States with vision impairment, 1 million with blindness. That’s a lot of people we’re leaving out. 

And, listen, I don’t think anyone in the industry is leaving blind people–or anyone, for that matter–out on purpose. This is just an example of what creative problem solving could do when we understand that a problem exists in the first place. There is more than curing blindness to solve for a blind social media user, but it’s not surprising that sometimes we don’t think of it.

Society has kind of conditioned us to forget, ignore, or unsee people outside of the status quo. The Ugly Laws–municipal ordinances that prohibited people with disabilities and disfigurements from appearing in public–were not fully repealed until the 1970s. And there are large swaths of the population who believe it’s more polite to say they don’t see color rather than work towards being anti-racist. Both of these examples exist on the premise that not seeing the difference will make the difference go away, whereas acknowledging the difference requires the seer to act on an injustice. To get creative.

The same goes for the work place. 

A friend of mine was recently laid off from his job because he was unable to be in-office for more than two days a week due to his disabling Long COVID. His company was requiring him to come in more often, not because his duties depended on it, but because being in-office fulltime was the normal thing to do. Rather than accommodate him creatively, they got rid of him.

Since 2023, return-to-office mandates have taken off, leaving many disabled people–some who were able to hold steady work for the first time during lockdowns thanks to work-from-home accommodations–in the dust. The gender wage gap has also increased since then, with women leaving the work force or taking pay cuts in order to balance home and work life. 

The sad part here is that we already saw and experienced the creative solution to these problems. Many places, including SuperBloom (hey girl), have adopted it–hybrid models with flexibility for people’s individual needs. But many companies are determined to return to normal, even if that means losing some of the most creative minds out there. 

Envisioning a world where our most vulnerable, marginalized, or forgotten are safe and visible is the most radical, most creative mindset you can have. But it doesn’t have to be–and shouldn’t, and isn’t–only possible for people on the outside. 

I was wrong when I was annoyed at my friend for constantly talking about the job he lost to illness. He deserved to shine a light on it as much as he wanted. And I wish I had been open to what it revealed before I became disabled myself. But, this worldview can be all of ours, identity aside, as long as we start thinking (and hiring, and making) with breaking norms in mind. You know, like creatives. 

Like with all civil rights movements–when we acknowledge, fight for, and include the most marginalized among us, we all benefit. In the most capitalistic of terms, that means our creative work gets better. It’s made by voices with worldviews that are different and more expansive than ours. It’s more thorough, fuller, interesting, deviant, eye-catching. It sees the world for how it really is and imagines something better. It pulls the weeds out of our archaic foundations and plants seeds for something new.

In the most human of terms, it means we get to be the kind of creatives, and people, we all claim to be. People who lean into the sun to unearth our unconscious biases. People who consider others, accommodate differences, and know that we are made better when we all have a chance to bloom.
